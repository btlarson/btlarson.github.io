About

I am a postdoc in Wallace Marshall’s lab at UCSF. I use microscopes, math, computation, and sometimes a bit of fieldwork to understand how cells control shape and movement to thrive in various environments. I am particularly interested in the regulation and evolution of complex cellular function in microbial eukaryotes (protists). I work at the interface of several fields including cell biology, biophysics, and evolutionary biology.

While I started undergrad at Reed College planning on majoring in philosophy, I eventually changed my plans after a course on the philosophy of biology convinced me of the beauty and power of the conceptual tools of physics. After a stint in Justin Taraska’s lab at the NIH doing a lot of computational image analysis, I joined the Biophysics PhD Program at UC Berkeley. There, I worked in Nicole King’s lab on the question of how animals evolved from unicellular ancestors by studying the cellular and biophysical mechanisms regulating 3D colony shape in choanoflagellates, the closest living relatives of animals.

In my postdoc at UCSF, I'm trying to understand the biophysical and computational principles by which cells control behavior by studying locomotion and sensorimotor activity in ciliates and amoebae. For more on my research interests, see Research and Publications.
